## 2. What We Believe

1. Digital harm is becoming legal harm.
   Deepfakes, scams, cyberstalking, account takeover, AI-generated defamation, fake evidence, and platform failures are no longer niche technical issues.

2. Most victims do not know what to preserve.
   People often delete chats, forward files until metadata is lost, take partial screenshots, or contact scammers again without a safety plan.

3. Courts and lawyers need better technical translation.
   The job is not to hype AI, but to make evidence, causation, harm, and uncertainty understandable.

4. AI should support humans, not replace legal judgment.
   AI can help with intake, evidence organization, timeline building, first-response guidance, and draft preparation. Licensed professionals and courts make legal conclusions.

5. The right fight is lawful, evidence-first, and human-reviewed.
   We fight through evidence, legal process, platform accountability, public education, and policy reform — not hack-back, surveillance, or vigilantism.

## 3. Why We Must Exist

The core problem:

> AI and digital harm now move faster than law, courts, platforms, and ordinary people can respond.

We exist because the gap is no longer only technical or legal. It is a combined failure of evidence, access, literacy, response speed, and policy.

### 3.1 The Law Is Too Slow for AI-Scale Harm

AI-generated harm can spread in minutes, while legal systems move in weeks, months, or years.

Examples:

- deepfake abuse can be copied and re-uploaded before a takedown process finishes
- voice clone scams can move money before the victim understands what happened
- AI-generated defamation can spread across platforms faster than evidence can be preserved
- fake screenshots, synthetic media, and manipulated chats can enter disputes before courts know how to evaluate them
- algorithmic decisions can harm people without a clear human decision-maker to question

Why this matters:

> If the first response is slow, the victim loses evidence, leverage, safety, and sometimes legal remedies.

### 3.2 Victims Often Lose Before Reaching a Lawyer or Court

Many people do not lose because they have no rights. They lose because they cannot turn digital harm into usable evidence.

Common failure points:

- deleting chats, files, posts, or account history out of fear or shame
- forwarding media until metadata and provenance are lost
- keeping screenshots without URLs, timestamps, account IDs, or context
- continuing to talk to scammers or abusers without a safety plan
- mixing facts, assumptions, emotions, and accusations in the first report
- sending lawyers or police a pile of unorganized files
- missing urgent platform, bank, employer, or account-recovery windows

Why this matters:

> People need a first-response layer before evidence disappears.

### 3.3 Lawyers and Courts Need a Technical Translation Layer

Many legal professionals can argue law, but AI/cyber evidence requires technical translation.

The system needs help explaining:

- what an artifact is
- where it came from
- whether it was preserved correctly
- what the metadata can and cannot prove
- whether a claim is fact, inference, or speculation
- what expert analysis is still needed
- how technical uncertainty should affect legal argument

Why this matters:

> Courts do not need AI hype. Courts need reliable explanations, provenance, limits, and relevance.

### 3.4 Platforms, Banks, Employers, and Police Often Fragment the Response

Digital harms often touch multiple institutions at once.

A single incident may require:

- platform takedown or preservation request
- bank or payment dispute
- account recovery
- employer or school report
- police report
- lawyer consultation
- regulator complaint
- mental-safety or physical-safety planning

Current problem:

- each institution asks for different evidence
- victims repeat the story many times
- timelines become inconsistent
- no one owns the whole picture
- urgent steps are missed

Why this matters:

> The victim needs one coherent case packet that can travel across institutions.

### 3.5 Cybersecurity, Forensics, Legal Practice, and Policy Are Too Separated

Today the relevant expertise is split across different worlds:

- lawyers know rights and procedure
- cyber defenders know attacks and logs
- forensic experts know evidence integrity
- AI researchers know model/system risk
- NGOs know victim support
- policymakers know reform channels

But AI-era harm crosses all of them at once.

Why this matters:

> A victim-centered organization must connect these fields instead of forcing victims to coordinate them alone.

### 3.6 Policy Without Case Evidence Is Too Abstract

AI policy often becomes vague when it is not grounded in real harm patterns.

We need case-informed policy evidence:

- what victims failed to preserve
- where platforms responded too slowly
- where banks, telcos, employers, or schools lacked procedure
- where courts struggled with synthetic evidence
- where current law has no practical remedy
- what reporting and preservation duties should exist

Why this matters:

> Real cases reveal the gap between legal theory and lived harm.

### 3.7 The Market Gap

Existing options are fragmented:

- legal tech often automates forms or gives generic legal information
- cybersecurity firms often focus on organizations, not everyday victims
- forensic labs are expensive and usually late in the process
- AI safety work often focuses on model governance, not individual legal harm
- victim-support organizations may lack technical evidence workflows
- platforms focus on internal policy, not victim-centered case preparation

Our role:

> We are the missing bridge between victim first response, digital evidence, legal advocacy, technical expertise, and policy reform.

### 3.8 The Reason to Build Now

The next wave of legal harm will include:

- cheap deepfakes
- real-time voice impersonation
- AI-personalized scams
- automated harassment
- fake evidence in disputes
- AI-generated reputational attacks
- agentic tool abuse
- algorithmic decisions that people cannot appeal effectively

If we wait for the law to catch up, people will keep losing evidence, safety, money, reputation, and rights.

Therefore, the company must exist to:

- help people respond before evidence disappears
- translate technical harm into legal action
- support lawyers and courts with clearer evidence
- build public literacy before scams scale further
- convert case patterns into better policy

North-star problem statement:

> People are being harmed by AI and digital systems faster than they can understand, preserve, prove, and fight back through lawful channels.

North-star answer:

> We help people not lose before the law has a chance to work.

## 4. Company Doctrine

We teach and build around five verbs:

### 4.1 Use

We teach people and professionals to use AI safely.

Allowed uses:

- understand incidents
- organize information
- prepare evidence
- draft reports for human review
- support lawyers, researchers, NGOs, journalists, and incident responders
- improve access to justice

Not allowed:

- impersonation
- deception
- unauthorized surveillance
- replacing licensed legal judgment
- creating or expanding harm

### 4.2 Prevent

We help people avoid harm before it happens.

Focus areas:

- deepfake awareness
- voice clone scam prevention
- phishing and scam literacy
- account security
- password managers, passkeys, MFA
- safe word and callback protocols
- secret hygiene
- prompt-injection awareness
- safe use of coding agents and AI tools

Goal:

> Give ordinary people practical digital immunity.

### 4.3 Protect

We support people who are at risk or already harmed.

We help them:

- preserve evidence
- avoid evidence contamination
- make a timeline
- separate fact, inference, and unknowns
- prepare a lawyer-ready brief
- prepare platform, police, regulator, or employer reports
- reduce panic and prevent avoidable mistakes

Principle:

> Protect people, preserve evidence, reduce harm.

### 4.4 Fight

“Fight” means lawful advocacy, not revenge or hack-back.

We fight:

- scams
- abuse
- harassment
- deepfake harms
- fraud
- platform negligence
- unfair automated decisions
- AI-generated evidence abuse

How we fight:

- evidence preservation
- legal argument
- court education
- expert support
- platform reporting
- regulatory complaints
- policy memos
- public education
- strategic litigation support

Hard boundary:

> We do not teach or support unauthorized access, credential theft, malware, evasion, doxxing, unlawful deanonymization, deepfake abuse, or hack-back.

### 4.5 Research

We research AI, cyber, forensic, and legal systems to improve defense and accountability.

Research topics:

- synthetic media risk
- AI-assisted social engineering
- digital evidence standards
- chain of custody
- forensic artifact handling
- legal intake automation
- defensive AI agents
- algorithmic discrimination
- platform accountability
- cybercrime response reform
- digital rights and victim support

Research rules:

- use synthetic data, authorized data, or lawful public sources
- prefer sandboxed environments
- document methods and limits
- avoid collecting unnecessary sensitive data
- separate evidence from inference
- publish responsibly

## 9. Defensive Forensic AI Product Concept

Working category:

> AI-assisted legal and digital evidence clinic.

Not:

> AI lawyer replacing a human lawyer.

Core promise:

> In 15 minutes, understand what happened, what to preserve, and what to bring to a lawyer, platform, police, employer, or regulator.

### 9.1 Incident Intake

Incident types:

- deepfake or impersonation
- scam or phishing
- hacked account
- online harassment
- fake evidence
- contract/payment dispute
- workplace digital evidence
- algorithmic decision harm
- unknown suspicious incident

### 9.2 Evidence Checklist

The system tells users:

- what not to delete
- what not to edit
- what original files to keep
- what screenshots are useful
- what metadata matters
- what URLs, IDs, timestamps, and headers to preserve
- when to stop interacting with the suspected attacker
- when to seek urgent help

### 9.3 Timeline Builder

The AI helps organize:

- what happened
- when it happened
- who was involved
- what evidence supports each claim
- what remains unknown
- what is inferred but not proven

### 9.4 Evidence Integrity Mode

Rules:

- original evidence is read-only
- working copies are separate
- hash files when appropriate
- record filename, size, source, and timestamp
- log AI actions
- never let AI modify evidence
- label confidence clearly

### 9.5 Legal Prep Summary

Output:

- issue summary
- known facts
- evidence list
- timeline
- key legal questions
- urgency and risk
- requested action
- open questions for lawyer or expert

### 9.6 Action Packs

Possible packs:

- lawyer consultation pack
- platform takedown/report pack
- police report prep
- regulator complaint draft
- employer/school incident report
- account recovery checklist
- bank/payment dispute prep

## 13. Safety and Trust Principles

### 13.1 Evidence First

Every important claim should trace back to:

- source file
- URL
- timestamp
- hash when appropriate
- user statement
- platform record
- transaction record
- log entry
- expert opinion

### 13.2 Fact / Inference / Unknown

Every report should separate:

- fact: directly supported by evidence
- inference: reasonable interpretation
- unknown: not proven yet
- speculation: avoid unless clearly labeled and useful

### 13.3 Human Review

AI outputs are drafts and organization aids.

Human review is required for:

- legal advice
- court submissions
- police/regulator reports
- expert forensic conclusions
- public accusations
- policy claims based on case data

### 13.4 Privacy and Data Minimization

Principles:

- collect only what is needed
- avoid storing raw sensitive material unless necessary
- separate identifying data from research where possible
- use explicit consent
- define retention and deletion rules
- protect minors and vulnerable users with stricter review

### 13.5 Trauma-Aware and Victim-Safe

The system should:

- avoid blaming language
- prioritize immediate safety
- warn before asking for sensitive details
- avoid forcing repeated retelling
- provide escalation routes
- support trusted-person involvement when appropriate

## 15. Operating Model

### 15.1 Consumer Path

1. User reports harm.
2. System triages incident type and urgency.
3. System gives safety and evidence-preservation steps.
4. User uploads or records authorized evidence metadata.
5. System builds timeline and evidence index.
6. System generates legal-prep brief and action pack.
7. Human reviewer/lawyer validates before formal use.

### 15.2 Lawyer Path

1. Lawyer receives messy digital evidence.
2. System indexes and summarizes artifacts.
3. System creates timeline and issue map.
4. System flags gaps, contradictions, and expert needs.
5. Lawyer decides legal strategy.
6. System helps draft court-friendly technical explanations.

### 15.3 Policy Path

1. Cases reveal repeated harm patterns.
2. Sensitive details are minimized or anonymized.
3. Patterns are synthesized into policy memos.
4. Proposals are prepared for regulators, lawmakers, platforms, or civil society.
5. Public education materials are produced.
